Outsource execution. Do not outsource judgment.

Ben looks back at how the YY Method formed: two Ys, Why and Why Not, a Git-like commit shape, a family dance, and a symbol that keeps returning through his kids and creative work.
The episode turns that origin story into a practical warning about AI, checks and balances, sympathetic resonance, and the need to keep human judgment in the loop.

**Keywords**: YY Method, AI and judgment, Why and Why Not, sympathetic resonance, creative method, checks and balances, personal journal podcast, Arc 2

<p>Okay, this is a big one.</p>
<p>When I developed the YY Method, I used AI a ton. I ran that thing through 2.3 versions before it became a professional asset. Even then, I retained the ability to continue developing it outside that engagement—in a nonprofessional setting, or really in whatever other way I want.</p>
<p>It’s funny that I have to stop and think about what the YY Method actually is.</p>
<p>The current structure—Capture, Why, Why Not, Commit, and Timestamp—was shaped heavily through work with AI, I believe.</p>
<p>But how did it start?</p>
<p>I want to rewind and not think about what it says today. I want to remember where it came from.</p>
<p>One of the fun things is that I have an entire journal filled with sketches, strange scribbles, and my really bad handwriting, all documenting how the YY Method formed in the beginning.</p>
<p>So picture this.</p>
<p>YY, of course, is my stuffed animal from when I was a kid. You know that if you’ve listened to this podcast.</p>
<p>He has a squeaky voice. It’s really just my voice played around with, but it’s how I spoke as YY when I was a kid for many years.</p>
<p>Then, one day, I looked at YY and thought, <em>Wouldn’t it be cool if he were famous someday?</em></p>
<p>I don’t really want to be that famous.</p>
<p>But what if YY was?</p>
<p>That would be pretty cool.</p>
<p>When my kids were growing up, I told them YY stories. We went on all kinds of adventures together. As they started getting older, I thought, <em>Wouldn’t it be cool if YY could have his own thing?</em></p>
<p>So I created the YY Method.</p>
<p>The first concept was actually me holding up my fingers like two Ys. You can see this on the homepage of the website right now.</p>
<p>They don’t look exactly like Ys unless you use your imagination, but there they were: two Ys pointing upward.</p>
<p>The Why and the Why Not.</p>
<p>I thought that was clever.</p>
<p>Then I got a little nerdier.</p>
<p>I wrote a Y, and then another Y with a zero beneath it: Why and Why Not.</p>
<p>Then I drew a large Y with nodes at the top, the middle, and the bottom.</p>
<p>I kept staring at it and thinking, <em>This looks familiar. What is this?</em></p>
<p>Then it hit me.</p>
<p>It looked like a Git commit.</p>
<p>I thought, <em>Dang. Look at that.</em></p>
<p>The Why and the Why Not looked like two branches flowing into Commit.</p>
<p>How nerdy is that?</p>
<p>I thought it was cool, so I showed my kids.</p>
<p>Then they turned it into a dance.</p>
<p>They went:</p>
<p>“Why? Why not? Measure. Commit.”</p>
<p>At the time, we were experimenting with words like <em>measure</em>, <em>merge</em>, and a few others. We tried all kinds of things. Some stuck, and some didn’t.</p>
<p>I don’t think the dance stuck.</p>
<p>But they thought the idea was really cool.</p>
<p>More recently, one of my kids put that Y symbol on a website called yysworld.com.</p>
<p>I hadn’t seen one of my kids use that original Y in months and months.</p>
<p>Then suddenly, there it was on the website.</p>
<p>I think it’s there to stay.</p>
<p>The funny thing is that the YY Method isn’t entirely about Why and Why Not.</p>
<p>It isn’t.</p>
<p>If you look at the Ys in YY, they resemble tuning forks.</p>
<p>We call him YY because, well, originally we called him “Why.” But then “Why Why” sounded like a strange name, so eventually it just became YY.</p>
<p>Now, take a piece of paper and grab a pencil.</p>
<p>Draw a Y, but round the top into a U shape, then extend a line downward.</p>
<p>Put another one next to it.</p>
<p>What do you have?</p>
<p>Two tuning forks.</p>
<p>I’m a musician, and I’m obviously a nerd, so I started putting those two things together.</p>
<p>What happens when you have two tuning forks resonating?</p>
<p>You get sympathetic resonance.</p>
<p>The sound waves interact. They overlap. Together, they create something that wouldn’t exist in exactly the same way if there were only one tuning fork.</p>
<p>But are the two forks the same?</p>
<p>Are they opposites?</p>
<p>Or are they simply different?</p>
<p>I think they’re simply different.</p>
<p>There isn’t necessarily anything mystical about it. You just have two tuning forks, and they can be tuned to any frequency.</p>
<p>The point, for me, is that their resonance interacts and creates something new.</p>
<p>That’s where the echo came back and told me that <em>YY &amp; Me</em> is a growing canon of echoes.</p>
<p>The image of those tuning forks creating sympathetic resonance hit me in that moment.</p>
<p>It was kind of cool.</p>
<p>Maybe it doesn’t mean very much.</p>
<p>At least, it doesn’t yet.</p>
<p>I’m still trying to understand what it all means.</p>
<p>Here is my prediction, and I could be completely wrong, but I’m putting it on the record:</p>
<p>Someday, someone will develop a theory that actually explains how the tuning-fork model of the YY Method works.</p>
<p>Maybe it won’t be me.</p>
<p>Maybe it will be one of my kids.</p>
<p>Maybe it will be a total stranger.</p>
<p>But I hope that someday it comes to life.</p>
<p>So where is this podcast going?</p>
<p>I think I wanted to share the YY Method with you because it now encapsulates the way I think, with or without AI.</p>
<p>And it really has helped me.</p>
<p>I’ve come up with some incredibly zany ideas that the Why Not branch has stepped in and crushed.</p>
<p>It said, essentially:</p>
<p>“No. Don’t do that.”</p>
<p>These were ideas I thought were great.</p>
<p>But they were wrong.</p>
<p>Or at least, they were wrong for me.</p>
<p>The central premise of the YY Method—and this is what I’ll leave you with—is this:</p>
<p><strong>Outsource execution. Do not outsource judgment.</strong></p>
<p>I came up with that phrase. AI did not give it to me.</p>
<p>It came from thinking about the branches of government and the importance of checks and balances.</p>
<p>I would caution anyone listening—probably just me—to keep that image in mind.</p>
<p>Once you introduce AI into your thinking, you need a check and balance.</p>
<p>So whatever you do, whatever you take from this podcast, and whatever you take from the YY Method, do not outsource your judgment.</p>
<p>Make sure you have a way to examine why you’re doing something.</p>
<p>Then stop for a moment and ask:</p>
<p><strong>Why not?</strong></p>
