What comes out when nothing is planned?

Ben records without a script and notices what changes when the polish is removed: the room, the time pressure, the car as a recording booth, and the sound of his own voice becoming easier to hear.
The episode becomes a practical test of whether YY and Me can stay human while AI remains close by as a tool, not the voice itself.

**Keywords**: improvisation, AI and voice, unscripted podcasting, creative process, human judgment, personal journal podcast, Arc 2

<p>This episode is going to be completely off the top of my head, without any drafting.</p>
<p>I want to see what comes out of my mouth when I have absolutely nothing planned.</p>
<p>For one thing, recording Episode Seven made me realize that my car is perfect for this.</p>
<p>The sound is dead. It has acoustic glass. Quite frankly, it’s better than my basement.</p>
<p>With the iPhone I have now, I think I’m going to record here from now on. It can be my little sanctuary away from all the crazy kids making noise all the time.</p>
<p>You can probably hear them in some of my YouTube videos. You can hear the kids walking around.</p>
<p>That gets me thinking: Is all that background noise annoying? Is it noticeable? Does anybody really care?</p>
<p>I could take the time to clean everything up and make it sound professional.</p>
<p>My brother and I made a CD a long time ago. We went through the process of learning sound engineering. We did the noise reduction, compression, normalization, trimming—all of it.</p>
<p>We made a nice CD, and I’m still proud of it.</p>
<p>But it took a long time.</p>
<p>Listen, when I was younger, I had a lot of time. Let’s not kid anybody.</p>
<p>If you’re in college, you’re lucky.</p>
<p>You think you’re busy now?</p>
<p>I don’t need to belabor the point.</p>
<p>Now I realize that I don’t have that much time.</p>
<p>I can’t spend twelve hours trying to get every episode exactly right. I don’t have twelve hours to rehearse every script.</p>
<p>So how am I supposed to make the difference I want to make in the world—or even just in the lives of my kids—if I don’t have time to clean everything up enough for them to listen?</p>
<p>Maybe it doesn’t matter.</p>
<p>Maybe they’ll listen anyway.</p>
<p>Or maybe they won’t.</p>
<p>Maybe they don’t care.</p>
<p>I honestly don’t know.</p>
<p>Even though I’ve said repeatedly that I’m making this for my kids, and also for builders and other people, I think the first consumer—and possibly the only consumer—is me.</p>
<p>You want to know what else is funny?</p>
<p>A lot of people don’t like the sound of their own voice. I don’t particularly like mine either.</p>
<p>But I’m noticing that the more I listen to myself talk, the easier it becomes for me to think.</p>
<p>Is that weird?</p>
<p>Maybe it’s a little weird.</p>
<p>AI doesn’t talk the way we talk.</p>
<p>In fact, if AI continues in the direction it’s going, I think all AI is eventually going to sound the same.</p>
<p>I was listening to a host on the <em>Everyday AI</em> podcast—I think that’s what it’s called. He talks a lot about AI and uses vocabulary that might sound unusual unless you also use AI.</p>
<p>To me, his vocabulary sounded completely normal.</p>
<p>Which probably means I’m on that spectrum too.</p>
<p>And that’s probably because I use AI so much.</p>
<p>So <em>YY &amp; Me</em> is becoming my opportunity to learn how not to use that language.</p>
<p>It’s teaching me not to depend on AI to write everything.</p>
<p>Because I have become dependent on it.</p>
<p>I use it for work all the time. I even use it sometimes to write emails.</p>
<p>I know I probably shouldn’t admit that, but it’s true.</p>
<p>If you notice that an email ends with “Best,” followed by a comma, it was probably written by AI—unless the person sincerely says “Best.”</p>
<p>Although AI wouldn’t say it unless a lot of people already did.</p>
<p>At least, I think that’s how it works.</p>
<p>So where is this episode going?</p>
<p>I guess <em>YY &amp; Me</em> needs to stay human.</p>
<p>I considered a few other options.</p>
<p>I thought about bringing in my synthetic voice clone. I thought about producing more episodes that were less polished, just to get my ideas out there.</p>
<p>In fact, I did clone my voice. I also made a collection of episodes drafted with AI.</p>
<p>But that might be another story for another day.</p>
<p>In the meantime, I guess improv has taught me that a lot can come out of my mouth when I don’t practice.</p>
<p>It may not be as consistent, clean, or censored as something produced with AI.</p>
<p>But every so often, I think it’s okay to unload.</p>
<p>That’s how we talk to one another.</p>
<p>It’s how we talk to our kids and our friends.</p>
<p>It would be incredibly strange if we had to look at our phones every time we wanted to talk to somebody.</p>
<p>So this is me continuing to teach myself how to speak without relying on AI.</p>
