The first echo has returned.

After nearly a year away, Ben reflects on why the podcast went quiet, what the first arc taught him, and why the next one has to be spoken more directly.
This episode turns toward repetition, memory, AI-assisted scaffolding, and the renewed decision to stay in charge of the voice.

**Keywords**: memory and repetition, AI and authorship, off the cuff podcasting, creative return, personal journal podcast, voice and judgment, music and AI

<div class="transcript-paper">
<p>It’s been almost a year since my last episode.</p>
<p>After writing, recording, and producing it—essentially all at once—I felt exhausted.</p>
<p>When I produce a podcast by myself, I have to do everything: concept, drafting, voicing, sound engineering, setting up the podcast, distributing it to all the platforms, and tagging the metadata.</p>
<p>There are so many steps.</p>
<p>And in case you weren’t familiar with the process, I just listed most of them.</p>
<p>That’s why I disappeared. It took too much time.</p>
<p>I was also struggling.</p>
<p>You can probably hear it in Episodes Four through Six. AI had become deeply involved in how I was learning to write these episodes.</p>
<p>I talked about authorship.</p>
<p>In the dream sequence of Episode Six, YY was the captain of the Author Ship. He reminded me that I was the captain of the actual authorship in Episode One.</p>
<p>Then those ideas started to blur together.</p>
<p>To make matters worse, I realized the podcast was taking time and attention that my family needed from me.</p>
<p>Here I was, building <em>YY &amp; Me: A Growing Canon of Echoes</em>.</p>
<p>But the echoes themselves were becoming so deafening that my target audience—my kids as adults, along with anyone else listening—was getting drowned out.</p>
<p>They needed my time now.</p>
<p>So I gave it up for a while.</p>
<p>But I’m back because it’s taken a year for the echo to bounce.</p>
<p>That first echo has returned, and I’ve learned a lot.</p>
<p>I’ve learned how to podcast. I understand the process now. I’ve listened to many different podcasts, and I’ve educated myself on what it takes to help a podcast find its audience.</p>
<p>I’ve also run some experiments.</p>
<p>I learned about diegetic sponsorship, for example.</p>
<p>The fact that the word *diegetic* is even being used in my podcast is a sign of AI, for sure. That word wasn’t in my vocabulary until AI came along.</p>
<p>But I understand what it means.</p>
<p>A diegetic sponsorship involves weaving a sponsor’s information—and what they want to communicate—into the narrative itself.</p>
<p>I did that on purpose.</p>
<p>Episode Six used something like that structure, although it wasn’t a paid sponsorship.</p>
<p>And it’s something AI still can’t figure out, even when I ask it to analyze the transcript.</p>
<p>Knowing that—and knowing where AI is today—I now understand that there are parts of what I do in this podcast that AI still can’t replace.</p>
<p>That gives me hope.</p>
<p>The echo also taught me a few things.</p>
<p>I love having the violin at the beginning and end of each episode. I’m going to continue doing that.</p>
<p>In fact, I’m going to experiment with using just one—the favorite of all the ones I used in the first arc.</p>
<p>It will be the same one every time because I think there’s power in memory and repetition.</p>
<p>There is so much power in repetition.</p>
<p>It powers spaced rehearsal in language apps. It powers AI through memory. Repetition powers practice.</p>
<p>That matters to me as a musician.</p>
<p>Now that I’m back, I also return with some new skills.</p>
<p>I was recently hired as a CTO, and I used AI alongside my technical judgment to build a company system from the ground up.</p>
<p>What began as theory is now supporting real work inside the company.</p>
<p>So I know AI works.</p>
<p>I know it’s extremely powerful when used in a way that preserves my judgment. I can return to that system, build it out, and remain in charge.</p>
<p>I’ve learned that podcasting can work similarly.</p>
<p>I can use AI to help write a script. I can give it the ideas and let it build the scaffolding. I can even let it draft the entire episode.</p>
<p>Then <em>YY &amp; Me</em> has taught me to put that script away.</p>
<p>I speak out loud and share the thoughts that come to me in the moment, without the scaffolding in front of me.</p>
<p>I don’t need it there anymore.</p>
<p>I’ve had enough practice—and enough repetition—that I think <em>YY &amp; Me</em>, for the second echo and the second arc, is going to be entirely off the cuff.</p>
<p>What does that mean?</p>
<p>It means I’m still going to plan. I’m still going to use AI to help me draft. I’ll probably read those drafts aloud and practice them.</p>
<p>But at the end of the day, a concept can enter the *YY &amp; Me* podcast only if I can articulate it out loud without help.</p>
<p>Which is what I’m doing today.</p>
<p>Right now.</p>
<p>And it feels great.</p>
<p>It feels clean.</p>
<p>It feels like I’m not being smoothed over, because what I’m saying is coming directly from my head in the moment.</p>
<p>That’s the spirit by which I want to steer this Author Ship from now on.</p>
<p>I also want to close this episode by saying that this is the first time I feel like we can depart from the harbor.</p>
<p>So far, we’ve been figuring out the ship.</p>
<p>I’ve been trying to determine what the cargo is, who’s manning the crew, and where we’re headed.</p>
<p>To be honest, I still don’t know where we’re headed.</p>
<p>All I know is that a new opportunity has appeared—one that connects this ship to something happening in AI and music at exactly the right moment.</p>
<p>What an awesome opportunity.</p>
<p>I’m not going to miss it.</p>
<p>Off we go.</p>
<p>This is <em>YY &amp; Me</em>.</p>
<p>Let’s go.</p>
