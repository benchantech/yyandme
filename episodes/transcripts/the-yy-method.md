# YY & Me, Episode 9: The YY Method

Okay, this is a big one.

When I developed the YY Method, I used AI a ton. I ran that thing through 2.3 versions before it became a professional asset. Even then, I retained the ability to continue developing it outside that engagement—in a nonprofessional setting, or really in whatever other way I want.

It’s funny that I have to stop and think about what the YY Method actually is.

The current structure—Capture, Why, Why Not, Commit, and Timestamp—was shaped heavily through work with AI, I believe.

But how did it start?

I want to rewind and not think about what it says today. I want to remember where it came from.

One of the fun things is that I have an entire journal filled with sketches, strange scribbles, and my really bad handwriting, all documenting how the YY Method formed in the beginning.

So picture this.

YY, of course, is my stuffed animal from when I was a kid. You know that if you’ve listened to this podcast.

He has a squeaky voice. It’s really just my voice played around with, but it’s how I spoke as YY when I was a kid for many years.

Then, one day, I looked at YY and thought, *Wouldn’t it be cool if he were famous someday?*

I don’t really want to be that famous.

But what if YY was?

That would be pretty cool.

When my kids were growing up, I told them YY stories. We went on all kinds of adventures together. As they started getting older, I thought, *Wouldn’t it be cool if YY could have his own thing?*

So I created the YY Method.

The first concept was actually me holding up my fingers like two Ys. You can see this on the homepage of the website right now.

They don’t look exactly like Ys unless you use your imagination, but there they were: two Ys pointing upward.

The Why and the Why Not.

I thought that was clever.

Then I got a little nerdier.

I wrote a Y, and then another Y with a zero beneath it: Why and Why Not.

Then I drew a large Y with nodes at the top, the middle, and the bottom.

I kept staring at it and thinking, *This looks familiar. What is this?*

Then it hit me.

It looked like a Git commit.

I thought, *Dang. Look at that.*

The Why and the Why Not looked like two branches flowing into Commit.

How nerdy is that?

I thought it was cool, so I showed my kids.

Then they turned it into a dance.

They went:

“Why? Why not? Measure. Commit.”

At the time, we were experimenting with words like *measure*, *merge*, and a few others. We tried all kinds of things. Some stuck, and some didn’t.

I don’t think the dance stuck.

But they thought the idea was really cool.

More recently, one of my kids put that Y symbol on a website called yysworld.com.

I hadn’t seen one of my kids use that original Y in months and months.

Then suddenly, there it was on the website.

I think it’s there to stay.

The funny thing is that the YY Method isn’t entirely about Why and Why Not.

It isn’t.

If you look at the Ys in YY, they resemble tuning forks.

We call him YY because, well, originally we called him “Why.” But then “Why Why” sounded like a strange name, so eventually it just became YY.

Now, take a piece of paper and grab a pencil.

Draw a Y, but round the top into a U shape, then extend a line downward.

Put another one next to it.

What do you have?

Two tuning forks.

I’m a musician, and I’m obviously a nerd, so I started putting those two things together.

What happens when you have two tuning forks resonating?

You get sympathetic resonance.

The sound waves interact. They overlap. Together, they create something that wouldn’t exist in exactly the same way if there were only one tuning fork.

But are the two forks the same?

Are they opposites?

Or are they simply different?

I think they’re simply different.

There isn’t necessarily anything mystical about it. You just have two tuning forks, and they can be tuned to any frequency.

The point, for me, is that their resonance interacts and creates something new.

That’s where the echo came back and told me that *YY & Me* is a growing canon of echoes.

The image of those tuning forks creating sympathetic resonance hit me in that moment.

It was kind of cool.

Maybe it doesn’t mean very much.

At least, it doesn’t yet.

I’m still trying to understand what it all means.

Here is my prediction, and I could be completely wrong, but I’m putting it on the record:

Someday, someone will develop a theory that actually explains how the tuning-fork model of the YY Method works.

Maybe it won’t be me.

Maybe it will be one of my kids.

Maybe it will be a total stranger.

But I hope that someday it comes to life.

So where is this podcast going?

I think I wanted to share the YY Method with you because it now encapsulates the way I think, with or without AI.

And it really has helped me.

I’ve come up with some incredibly zany ideas that the Why Not branch has stepped in and crushed.

It said, essentially:

“No. Don’t do that.”

These were ideas I thought were great.

But they were wrong.

Or at least, they were wrong for me.

The central premise of the YY Method—and this is what I’ll leave you with—is this:

**Outsource execution. Do not outsource judgment.**

I came up with that phrase. AI did not give it to me.

It came from thinking about the branches of government and the importance of checks and balances.

I would caution anyone listening—probably just me—to keep that image in mind.

Once you introduce AI into your thinking, you need a check and balance.

So whatever you do, whatever you take from this podcast, and whatever you take from the YY Method, do not outsource your judgment.

Make sure you have a way to examine why you’re doing something.

Then stop for a moment and ask:

**Why not?**
